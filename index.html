What Is Hellenistic Astrology?

If you've never studied astrology before, this is the right place to start. No prior knowledge needed — just curiosity.

I

The Big Idea

Hellenistic astrology is one of the oldest forms of astrology in the Western world, developed over 2,000 years ago. It's based on the idea that the positions of the Sun, Moon, and planets at the exact moment and place of your birth create a unique map — called a birth chart — that describes your personality, life themes, and potential.

II

What's a Birth Chart?

Think of a birth chart as a snapshot of the sky at the moment you were born. It shows exactly where each planet was positioned — in which zodiac sign, and in which "house" (area of life). The chart is a circle divided into 12 sections, like a clock face, with symbols for each planet placed inside it.

III

The Three Things That Matter Most

When reading a chart, astrologers look at three placements first: your Sun sign (your core identity — what most horoscopes are based on), your Moon sign (your emotional nature), and your Rising sign / Ascendant (how you appear to others, and the foundation through which your whole chart is read in Hellenistic astrology).

Quick Glossary — Words You'll See on This Site

Natal Chart Another name for your birth chart — "natal" simply means "of birth"
Ascendant (ASC) The zodiac sign rising on the eastern horizon at your birth — the single most important point in Hellenistic astrology
Planet In Hellenistic astrology: the Sun, Moon, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn — the 7 wandering stars visible to the naked eye
Sign One of 12 sections of the zodiac belt (Aries, Taurus, etc.) — tells you the style or quality of a planet's expression
House One of 12 sections of the chart divided by the horizon and meridian — tells you which area of life a planet is activating
Aspect A geometric angle between two planets — shows how they interact and influence each other
Step by Step

How to Calculate Your Birth Chart

You don't need to do any math — free websites handle all the calculations. Here's exactly how to get and read your chart using the Hellenistic method.

1

Gather Your Birth Information

You'll need three pieces of information:

  • Date of birth — day, month, and year
  • Time of birth — as precise as possible (look on your birth certificate). Even an approximate time helps, but the exact minute matters most for your Rising sign.
  • Place of birth — the city or town where you were born
TipIf you don't know your birth time, try requesting your birth certificate or asking a parent. Without a birth time, you won't be able to accurately determine your Rising sign or house placements — the two things Hellenistic astrology relies on most heavily.
2

Go to a Free Chart Calculator

Use one of these trusted, free websites to generate your chart:

  • Astro.com — the most widely used by serious students. Go to "Free Horoscopes" → "Extended Chart Selection" for full options.
  • Astro-seek.com — beginner-friendly interface with clear labels and explanations.
Hellenistic NoteModern astrology often uses "Placidus" houses by default. For Hellenistic astrology, always select Whole Sign Houses as your house system — the method used by ancient Greek astrologers, where each house occupies one complete zodiac sign. On Astro.com, look for the "Houses" dropdown under Extended Chart Selection.
3

Enter Your Information & Read the Chart

Fill in your date, time, and place of birth, then click "Calculate." You'll see a circular wheel with symbols inside — that's your birth chart. Here's how to make sense of it:

⊕The circle is divided into 12 wedge-shaped sections — these are your Houses (areas of life)
☿The symbols scattered inside the wheel are Planets, placed in the sign and house they occupied at your birth
→The horizontal line through the middle is the horizon. The left point (marked ASC or AC) is your Ascendant — your Rising sign
↑The vertical line is the meridian. The top point (MC) is your Midheaven — associated with career and public life
4

Find Your Three Most Important Placements

After generating your chart, locate these three points first. They're the foundation of everything else in Hellenistic astrology:

→ ASC
Your Rising Sign

The zodiac sign at the far left of your chart wheel, marked "ASC" or "AC." In Hellenistic astrology this is the single most important point — it sets the first house and determines how the entire chart is structured.

☉
Your Sun Sign

Find the ☉ symbol in the wheel. The zodiac sign it sits in is your Sun sign — what most people know as their "star sign." In Hellenistic astrology the Sun represents the soul, vital spirit, and life purpose.

☽
Your Moon Sign

Find the ☽ symbol. The Moon moves quickly (changing signs every ~2.5 days), so your exact birth time matters here. Your Moon sign describes your body, instincts, and emotional inner world.

5

Read the Planet Table Alongside the Wheel

Most chart websites display a table listing each planet, the sign it's in, the house it occupies, and its degree. This table is often easier to read than the wheel itself. Here's a simple example of what you might see:

The Foundation

Origins & History

𓂀

Egyptian Roots

The decanic system — dividing the zodiac into 36 segments of 10° each — emerged from ancient Egyptian temple ceilings and star calendars. Egypt's Hellenistic capital, Alexandria, became the cradle of the new synthesis.

𒀭

Babylonian Inheritance

Centuries of meticulous astronomical records from Mesopotamia provided the planetary observations and omens that formed the empirical backbone of Hellenistic astrology. The zodiac itself was a Babylonian invention.

⚛

Greek Philosophy

Stoic cosmology, Platonic metaphysics, and Aristotelian physics gave astrology its theoretical framework. The concept of sympathy (συμπάθεια) — the interconnectedness of all things — underpinned the whole system.

⚖

The Synthesis

Around the 1st–2nd century BCE, the nativity (birth chart) became the primary focus. This shift from mundane omen interpretation to individual fate reading marks the birth of horoscopic astrology as we know it.

The Seven Wanderers

The Classical Planets

Hellenistic astrology recognized seven planets — the "wandering stars" visible to the naked eye. Each carried rich mythological, physical, and temperamental meanings.

☉
The Sun
Ἥλιος · Helios
Sect: Day Domicile: Leo Exaltation: Aries

The luminary of the day sect, the Sun represents the soul, vital spirit, kings, fathers, and public honor. Its position in the chart reveals one's identity and life purpose.

☽
The Moon
Σελήνη · Selene
Sect: Night Domicile: Cancer Exaltation: Taurus

Luminary of the night sect, the Moon governs the body, instincts, mothers, the common people, and all things moist and changeable. She reflects the soul's receptive nature.

☿
Mercury
Ἑρμῆς · Hermes
Sect: Both Domicile: Gemini/Virgo Exaltation: Virgo

The messenger and trickster, Mercury rules the rational mind, speech, writing, commerce, and dexterity. As the only planet of both sects, it adapts its nature to context.

♀
Venus
Ἀφροδίτη · Aphrodite
Sect: Night Domicile: Taurus/Libra Exaltation: Pisces

The lesser benefic, Venus bestows beauty, love, pleasure, arts, and harmony. In a night chart she is especially powerful, bringing grace and good fortune to matters of affection.

♂
Mars
Ἄρης · Ares
Sect: Day Domicile: Aries/Scorpio Exaltation: Capricorn

The lesser malefic, Mars governs conflict, courage, surgery, fire, and iron. In a day chart his heat is tempered; he represents the will to act and the capacity for destruction.

♃
Jupiter
Ζεύς · Zeus
Sect: Day Domicile: Sagittarius/Pisces Exaltation: Cancer

The greater benefic, Jupiter signifies expansion, justice, philosophy, wealth, and divine favor. He is the most fortunate planet, bringing abundance wherever he casts his rays.

♄
Saturn
Κρόνος · Kronos
Sect: Night Domicile: Capricorn/Aquarius Exaltation: Libra

The greater malefic, Saturn rules time, limitation, old age, agriculture, and grief. Yet he also governs wisdom earned through hardship and the discipline that leads to mastery.

The Twelve Places

The Houses (Topoi)

The twelve houses — called topoi (τόποι, "places") in Greek — divide the celestial sphere relative to the horizon. Each house governs a distinct sphere of earthly life.

I
The Helm / Ascendant

Life, the body, character, early years. The most powerful angle — where the soul enters the world.

II
The Gate of Hades

Resources, livelihood, possessions. A weak cadent house, traditionally inauspicious.

III
The Goddess

Siblings, short journeys, communications, religion. Moon-like in its cyclical associations.

IV
The Subterranean Pivot

Parents, home, land, lineage, the end of matters. An angle of deep foundational importance.

V
Good Fortune

Children, pleasure, creativity, erotic love. The Joy of Venus — a place of delight and abundance.

VI
Bad Fortune

Illness, servitude, enemies, animals. The Joy of Mars — a difficult place of hardship.

VII
The Setting Place

Marriage, partnerships, open enemies, contracts. The western angle — powerful and relational.

VIII
The Idle Place

Death, inheritance, surgery, the resources of others. Traditionally weak but intensely karmic.

IX
The God

Philosophy, religion, long journeys, dreams, divination. The Joy of the Sun — exalted matters.

X
The Midheaven

Career, fame, public life, authority. The highest angle — the peak of solar power.

XI
Good Spirit

Friends, hopes, gifts of fortune, patrons. The Joy of Jupiter — a place of beneficence.

XII
Bad Spirit

Exile, hidden enemies, imprisonment, grief. The Joy of Saturn — a place of restriction and sorrow.

The Craft

Key Techniques

The most fundamental organizing principle of Hellenistic astrology. A chart is either a day chart (Sun above the horizon) or a night chart (Sun below). Day sect planets (Sun, Jupiter, Saturn) are stronger and more constructive in day charts; night sect planets (Moon, Venus, Mars) perform better at night. Mercury adapts to whichever luminary it rises with. Sect determines whether a planet's natural energy expresses harmoniously or creates friction.

Hellenistic astrology used five aspects: the conjunction (0°), sextile (60°), square (90°), trine (120°), and opposition (180°). These were understood as geometric relationships between zodiacal signs — signs that "see" each other. Signs in trine share the same element (harmonious); signs in square are three signs apart (tense); signs in opposition are enemies yet aware of each other. Conjunctions blend two planets' natures completely.

Lots (also called Arabic Parts) are sensitive points calculated from the relationship between two planets or points and projected from the Ascendant. The most important is the Lot of Fortune (☽ – ☉ + Asc by night; ☉ – ☽ + Asc by day), which indicates the body and material circumstances of life. The Lot of Spirit (its complement) indicates the soul and actions taken through free will. There were dozens of specialized lots for everything from marriage to injury.

Hellenistic astrology excelled at chronocrator (time-lord) systems — techniques for determining which planets govern specific periods of life. The most famous is Annual Profections: the Ascendant advances one house per year, and the ruler of that house becomes the year's lord. Zodiacal Releasing (from the Lots of Fortune and Spirit) produces major and minor periods of activation. The Firdaria and Decennials offer further layers of timing.

A planet's strength is assessed through its essential dignities — its relationship to the sign it occupies. Domicile: a planet in its own sign is most powerful (Saturn in Capricorn). Exaltation: a sign of elevated expression (Sun in Aries). Triplicity: elemental affinity by sect. Bounds (Terms): five unequal divisions of each sign. Face (Decan): 10° divisions. Planets in their own dignities act "at home"; planets in detriment or fall are weakened.

Beyond aspects, planets can be bonified (strengthened) or maltreated (harmed) through their relationship to other planets. A planet bonified by Jupiter via conjunction or trine gains benefic influence. A planet under the beams of the Sun (within 15°) is said to be "combust" and weakened. Cazimi (within 16′ of the exact solar center) is instead a place of great honor — the planet sits in the "heart of the Sun."

The Masters

Key Figures

Π

Ptolemy

c. 100–170 CE · Alexandria

Author of the Tetrabiblos (Τετράβιβλος), the most influential astrological text of antiquity. Ptolemy rationalized astrology within an Aristotelian framework, emphasizing natural causation. His work became the standard reference throughout the medieval world.

Β

Vettius Valens

c. 120–175 CE · Antioch

Author of the nine-book Anthology (Ἀνθολόγια), the most encyclopedic surviving Hellenistic text. A working astrologer who shared real client charts, Valens is the primary source for time-lord techniques including Zodiacal Releasing and Annual Profections.

Δ

Dorotheus of Sidon

1st c. CE · Sidon

Author of the Carmen Astrologicum, a five-book verse treatise on nativities, katarchic (electional) astrology, and horary questions. His work, preserved largely through Arabic translation, is the foundational text for natal and event astrology.

Π

Paulus Alexandrinus

4th c. CE · Alexandria

Author of the Introductory Matters (Εἰσαγωγικά), an accessible introductory text that preserves many techniques from earlier Hellenistic sources. Paulus is invaluable for understanding the standard curriculum of late antique astrological education.

Η

Hephaistio of Thebes

5th c. CE

Author of the Apotelesmatika, a three-book compilation that drew heavily on Dorotheus, Ptolemy, and others. Hephaistio is an important late source who preserves portions of earlier works now lost, particularly on inceptional (katarchic) astrology.

Μ

Manilius

1st c. CE · Rome

Author of the Astronomica, the earliest surviving complete astrological text in Latin. Written in elegant hexameter verse during the reign of Augustus and Tiberius, Manilius' poem presents a Stoic cosmos in which astrology reveals the divine order woven into all things.
